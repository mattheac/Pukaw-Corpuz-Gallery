ABOUT

PUKAW explores the powerful role of art in the 19th-century Philippine reformist movement. This online gallery showcases how paintings were used as strategic propaganda to challenge colonial rule and foster a sense of national pride.

This gallery features the works of trailblazing artists who navigated the tension between European influence and Filipino identity, while also highlighting the hidden political subtexts that helped shape a nation. Step inside to see how beauty and bravery merged on canvas to demand a better future.

ABSTRACT

The late Spanish colonial period in the Philippines (1880–1898) was characterized by profound social, political, and cultural upheaval. Driven by a growing awareness of colonial hierarchy and systematic oppression, the ilustrados promoted nationalism and patriotism through art, literature, and socio-political reform movements.

During this period, visual art evolved beyond religious and decorative functions. It became a powerful medium for self-expression and political resistance. Works by Juan Luna and Félix Resurrección Hidalgo gained international recognition and became symbols of Filipino intellect and identity.

While much scholarship focuses on reformist writings and the Propaganda Movement, the revolutionary role of visual art is often overlooked. These artworks reveal how cultural production awakened collective consciousness, fostered nationalist sentiment, and inspired revolutionary imagination.

Through symbolic depictions of suffering, colonial oppression, and cultural pride, these paintings contributed to the revolutionary spirit that culminated in the Philippine Revolution of 1896.
