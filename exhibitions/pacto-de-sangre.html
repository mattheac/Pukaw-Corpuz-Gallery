Juan Luna • 1886

El Pacto de Sangre

Context

Painted in 1886, El Pacto de Sangre depicts the 1565 blood compact between Datu Sikatuna and Miguel López de Legazpi—an event often framed as the beginning of Spanish-Filipino relations.

By the late 19th century, this event had been reinterpreted and narraturized as part of emerging Filipino historical consciousness and identity formation. The painting was created under colonial patronage expectations and intended for official and public display, reinforcing narratives of early cooperation.

Its audience included Spanish officials and the European public, as well as educated Filipinos engaged in reformist discourse, who saw in the canvas a legalistic foundation for their own modern demands.

Visual and Semiotic Analysis

Observe the careful geometry and power dynamics depicted in Luna’s portrayal of this founding ritual.

Key Visual Element

Centralized composition on the blood compact

Possible Interpretation:
In a semiotic sense, this focus may suggest unity and mutual agreement, placing the ritual at the heart of the relationship.
Key Visual Element

Triangular arrangement of figures

Possible Interpretation:
The stable geometry may convey harmony and equality, reinforcing the idea of a diplomatic and orderly encounter rather than a conquest.
Key Visual Element

Numerical imbalance between officials and Sikatuna

Possible Interpretation:
The presence of multiple Spanish figures against a lone Filipino leader may reflect an underlying asymmetry in power structures.

Luna uses light and structure to define the "sacred alliance" between two nations.

Historical Impact

The Myth of Equal Treaty

To the Filipino ilustrados in Europe, El Pacto de Sangre was far more than a historical recreation; it was a diplomatic proof of concept. Nationalist thinkers like Marcelo H. del Pilar and José Rizal interpreted the ritual as a "contractual agreement between equals."

They argued that by drinking the mixture of wine and blood, Sikatuna and Legazpi had entered into a sacred alliance of friendship and assimilation. This painting affirmed their demand for equal rights, allowing them to argue that Filipinos were not conquered subjects, but partners who had voluntarily sworn loyalty in exchange for modernization and inclusion.

From Broken Pact to Revolutionary Justice

While the work began as a symbol of the reformist stage of nationalism, it ultimately provided the moral logic for the revolution. According to historical analysis, the perceived violation of this "blood oath" by Spain gave Filipinos the legal and moral right to withdraw from the pact.

This transition moved the national consciousness from asking for favors to demanding independence as a matter of justice. By the time Andrés Bonifacio and the Katipunan launched the Revolution in 1896, they used the same symbol of the blood oath (Sandugo) to unify the people, transforming Luna's depiction of an ancient alliance into a modern mandate for a sovereign nation.
